Master Scenario Event List (MSEL)
A DPRK-affiliated cyber actor, operating under direction of the Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB), has conducted a multi-stage intrusion into V2X's LOGCAP life support management systems at Camp Humphreys. The attack exploits a zero-day vulnerability in the contractor's facilities management platform — the same system that controls food service scheduling, power distribution monitoring, water operations, and vehicle maintenance tracking for all ~36,000 personnel on the installation. The attack is timed to coincide with the annual US-ROK Ulchi Freedom Shield exercise, when the installation is at peak personnel density and operational tempo. The adversary's goal is not destruction but disruption: degrade life support confidence, force 8th Army into defensive posture, and demonstrate USFK's contractor-dependent vulnerability to Seoul and Washington.
ILS2 Context
SOCPAC's Integrated Logistics Support 2 (ILS2) contract — TOR 47QFCA26R0025, a ~$180–220M CPAF OASIS+ vehicle supporting SOCPAC and USINDOPACOM — is currently in pre-proposal development with proposals due September 2026. ILS2 is explicitly designed to provide the kind of agile, theater-wide logistics support that LOGCAP cannot: rapid-response, special operations-oriented, commercially-leveraged supply chain and life support across the INDOPACOM AOR. This exercise will surface throughout whether ILS2's structure, scope, and contractor team are positioned to fill the gaps this scenario exposes.

Exercise Objectives
Test senior leader decision-making when life support — not just logistics data — is at risk; assess understanding of LOGCAP contractor dependency in a near-peer threat environment; surface gaps in COOP planning for a geographically isolated overseas installation; evaluate combined command coordination with ROK-US CFC; identify contracting authority limitations when the contracting office (ACC-Rock Island) is CONUS-based and 14 time zones away.
Unique Humphreys Friction Points
(1) Camp Humphreys is a SOFA-governed installation — ROK sovereign law applies to contractor cyber incidents in ways it does not CONUS. (2) LOGCAP at Humphreys covers life support (food, water, power, billeting), not just logistics — the stakes of system failure are immediate and human. (3) The CFC dual-hat command structure means USFK/8th Army decisions have immediate ROK Combined Forces implications. (4) ACC-Rock Island contracting authority is operating at H-14 time zone offset — the Contracting Officer may be asleep when the crisis breaks.
Key Tensions to Surface
(1) How much of the attack do you share with ROK CFC, and when? (2) Can you isolate V2X systems without triggering a LOGCAP performance failure that creates a separate legal liability? (3) Who authorizes emergency food/water contingency measures — does that require declaration of an emergency under SOFA? (4) How do you prevent adversary information operations from exploiting visible life-support disruption on the peninsula?

FIRST INDICATION — Anomalous System Behavior During Peak Exercise Tempo
H+0:00 – H+0:45
▶
Situation — H+0:00, 0215 Local (Korea)
It is the third day of Ulchi Freedom Shield. Camp Humphreys is at 108% normal personnel density — the installation is hosting 8th Army's warfighting HQ, a rotational BCT, and multiple allied liaison teams. At 0215 local, V2X's facilities management platform begins producing erratic outputs: dining facility scheduling software shows conflicting meal count authorizations across three DFACs, the power distribution monitoring dashboard shows phantom alerts on circuits that physical inspection confirms are nominal, and the vehicle maintenance tracking system marks 22 vehicles as deadline with no associated work orders. The V2X night shift supervisor assumes a server issue and begins standard restart procedures. At 0248, the platform crashes entirely and fails to restart. 36,000 people wake up in four hours expecting breakfast.
Injects
INJECT 1-A [H+0:10] — V2X's Korea-based IT administrator identifies outbound data exfiltration in the crash logs — approximately 4.7GB of facilities management data, including power grid schematics, water system diagrams, DFAC capacity records, and vehicle fleet accountability data, transmitted to an external IP in the 30 minutes before the crash. The IP resolves to a commercial cloud provider with known RGB-associated indicators.

Situation — H+96, Day 5
V2X has restored approximately 85% of system functionality following a full forensic rebuild. Manual procedures bridged critical life-support gaps — the installation did not miss a meal service after Day 1, water supply was uninterrupted after the DPW intervention, and no Ulchi Freedom Shield exercise events were cancelled. However, the incident has had significant downstream effects: ROK press has published multiple stories sourced from Korean national contractor employees; the ROK National Assembly Defense Committee has requested a briefing; INDOPACOM has directed an assessment of LOGCAP contractor cybersecurity across all INDOPACOM installations; and the NSC has asked whether the LOGCAP contract model for overseas installations requires structural revision. V2X's contract expires in June 2030. The question now is what changes before then.

Strategic Context — Post-Incident, Day 14
The Humphreys incident has catalyzed a theater-wide conversation that was already underway: INDOPACOM's logistics architecture is dangerously concentrated in a single LOGCAP vehicle, operated by a single contractor, on a single platform, with no resilient alternative in theater. The ILS2 contract — TOR 47QFCA26R0025, a ~$180–220M CPAF OASIS+ vehicle supporting SOCPAC and USINDOPACOM — was already in pre-proposal development when the attack occurred. It is now the most scrutinized acquisition in the INDOPACOM AOR. SOCPAC, USINDOPACOM J4, HQDA G4, and the NSC all want to understand: does ILS2 as currently scoped solve the problem the Humphreys attack exposed? And what needs to change before proposals open in September 2026?
